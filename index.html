Reflection 1: Apply Principles of Sustainability to Create Viable Systems

During my studies, one of the most formative experiences related to sustainability was my involvement in an Engineering Without Borders (EWB) project focused on designing a solar–hydrogen energy system for an Indigenous community. The aim of the project was to propose a clean, reliable alternative to diesel-based power generation while accounting for environmental, social, and logistical constraints.

At the beginning of the project, I felt motivated and excited by the opportunity to work on a system that could have a positive long-term impact. However, I also felt uncertain about whether advanced technologies such as hydrogen fuel cells were realistic for a remote community setting. I was concerned that proposing a technically efficient system might overlook practical issues such as maintenance, transport, and long-term usability.

I realised that my initial assumptions were heavily influenced by a technical mindset that prioritised efficiency and performance. I believed that a more advanced system would automatically be a better solution. As the project progressed, this belief was challenged when our research highlighted the importance of scalability, community involvement, and long-term resource management in sustainable engineering solutions.

One of the key difficulties we encountered was balancing environmental sustainability with system complexity. While hydrogen fuel cells offered higher efficiency and lower emissions, they also introduced challenges related to infrastructure, training, and cost. Recognising these difficulties required us to critically assess whether our design truly supported long-term sustainability rather than just short-term technical success.

To broaden my perspective, I engaged with sustainability frameworks discussed in engineering literature and course materials, particularly the principles outlined by Dowling et al. (2016). Concepts such as conserving resources and consulting stakeholders helped me understand that sustainability extends beyond environmental impact to include social and economic viability. This shifted my focus toward designing systems that could be maintained, adapted, and understood by the community over time.

This experience taught me that sustainable engineering is fundamentally about creating systems that endure, not just systems that perform well. In the future, I intend to approach sustainability by more actively considering stakeholder needs, knowledge transfer, and lifecycle impacts alongside technical efficiency. I will also aim to question my assumptions earlier in the design process to ensure that proposed solutions are viable, responsible, and context-appropriate.

Reference

Dowling, D., Carew, A., & Hadgraft, R. (2016). Engineering your future: An Australasian guide (3rd ed.). John Wiley & Sons.

Reflection 2: Professional Practice within Intercultural and Global Contexts

A significant learning experience related to professional practice in intercultural and global contexts occurred during my involvement in the Engineering Without Borders project, where the design task required sensitivity to the needs of an Indigenous community. The project emphasised that engineering decisions do not occur in isolation and must consider cultural, social, and historical contexts.

Initially, I felt confident contributing from a technical perspective but uncertain about how to appropriately engage with cultural considerations that I was less familiar with. I was aware that making assumptions about community needs or priorities could result in solutions that were technically sound but socially ineffective. This uncertainty made me more cautious in how I approached design decisions and group discussions.

I recognised that my personal background and engineering training had shaped a problem-solving approach that prioritised optimisation and standardisation. This belief was challenged when it became clear that solutions suitable for urban or industrial contexts may not translate effectively to remote or culturally distinct communities. A key difficulty was ensuring that our design did not impose external values or overlook community autonomy.

To address this, our team relied on research, stakeholder reports, and guidance provided by EWB to gain a deeper understanding of the community's cultural and practical context. Considering perspectives beyond our own, including social and ethical dimensions, helped me appreciate the importance of respectful consultation and culturally informed design. This aligned with professional engineering expectations around ethical practice and global responsibility.

Through this process, I learned that effective engineering practice in intercultural contexts requires humility, active listening, and adaptability. Technical expertise alone is insufficient if it is not supported by cultural awareness and ethical judgment. In future professional practice, I intend to engage more deliberately with diverse perspectives, seek guidance when working in unfamiliar contexts, and design solutions that respect local knowledge and values while meeting engineering objectives.
